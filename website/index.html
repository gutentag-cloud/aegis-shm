Aegis

Physics-Informed Structural Damage Localization

Find hidden damage in a bridge
with a handful of sensors.

Aging beams and bridges develop invisible internal damage — local losses of stiffness — long before they fail. Dense sensor networks can find it, but they are expensive. Aegis embeds the governing equation of the structure directly into a neural network, so it can localize and quantify damage from only a few sparse, noisy deflection measurements.

① Physics, not just data

The Euler–Bernoulli beam equation is baked into the loss. The network cannot return a physically impossible structure.

② Sparse & noisy-robust

Works with a handful of sensors and realistic measurement noise, where classical curvature methods break down.

③ Continuous damage map

Outputs a full stiffness field EI(x) — location and severity — not a single yes/no alarm.

How it works

Aegis solves an inverse problem: given how the beam bends under known loads, infer the stiffness field that explains it. The panel below runs the real beam physics live in your browser — move the damage and watch the structure respond.

1

Known smooth loads q(x) are applied; sensors record the deflection at a few points.

2

A neural network represents the unknown stiffness field EI(x), initialized to a healthy beam.

3

An exact, differentiable beam solver turns any EI(x) into a predicted deflection — so the physics always holds.

4

Sparsity + total-variation priors keep the recovered damage localized and clean.

∂²/∂x² [ EI(x) · ∂²w/∂x² ] = q(x)
Euler–Bernoulli equation — embedded directly in the training loss

About this project

Aegis was built as a self-contained, reproducible research artifact for the International Science and Engineering Fair (ISEF), engineering — physics.

Reproducible

Every number on this site is produced by the code in this repository from exact, physics-based data — no proprietary datasets.

Impact

Cheaper structural-health monitoring for aging bridges and buildings, and faster post-earthquake triage.

Honest scope

Validated on 1-D Euler–Bernoulli beams in simulation; the discussion section lays out the path to real structures.
